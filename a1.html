To get each of the LEDs to blink independently, I used 3 different power sources the Arduino so that I could code each one to turn on/off separately for different lengths of time.

Circuit Image

For my circuit, I used 3 red LEDs and 3 220 Ohm resistors. I chose 220 Ohms because I calculated that the LEDs being on at the ideal 20 mA current would require 160 Ohm resistors which I did not have. 220 Ohms resulted in 14 mA current, still sufficiently turning on the lights.

As per the schematic, each LED has its own power source connected to its anode and its cathode goes to ground to complete the circuit. 

        
void setup() {
  pinMode(12, OUTPUT); //initializes pin 12 as output
  pinMode(7, OUTPUT); //initializes pin 7 as output
  pinMode(4, OUTPUT); //initializes pin 4 as output
}

void loop() {
  digitalWrite(12, HIGH); //turns LED connected to pin 12 on with high voltage
  delay(80); //pin 12 LED waits for 8/100 of a second
  digitalWrite(12, LOW); //pin 12 LED turns off with low voltage
  delay(80); //pin 12 LED waits for 8/100 of a second

  digitalWrite(7, HIGH); //turns LED connected to pin 7 on with high voltage
  delay(300);//pin 7 LED waits for 3/10 of a second
  digitalWrite(7, LOW); //pin 7 LED turns off with low voltage
  delay(300);//pin 7 LED waits for 3/10 of a second

  digitalWrite(4, HIGH); //turns LED connected to pin 4 on with high voltage
  delay(200);//pin 4 LED waits for 2/10 of a second
  digitalWrite(4, LOW); //pin 4 LED turns off with low voltage
  delay(200);//pin 4 LED waits for 2/10 of a second
}

Arduino Code Sketch

In the code setup, I initialized each of the pins I wanted to use as a power source. Then in the loop, I cycled through each pin making the LED connected to it turn on for a fraction of a second, then wait before the next one turned on.
